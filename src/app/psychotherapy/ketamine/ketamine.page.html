Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy

I am excited to be certified to offer Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy.

Although Ketamine is known as an anesthesia, it is now FDA approved for the treatment of depression, alcohol and substance dependence, food addiction, OCD, PTSD and other trauma related disorders.

Although it is not impactful for everyone, and to be clear it is not a cure, Ketamine treatment is opening doors to breakthroughs and healing transformations, as it exerts antidepressant effects by enhancing neuroplasticity, (meaning, the ability of brain cells to form new connections with one another and new pathways.) The glutamate pathway of ketamine is very different from that used by other psychiatric dugs, which is part of what makes its impact unlike other forms of treatment.

With ketamine assisted psychotherapy, the recommended treatment involves 5 or 6 administrations of ketamine, once a week or every other week, with an integration session following each session or every other session. This is a very different approach than the "ketamine infusion clinics" in which one goes for 6 or more weekly or bi-weekly “infusion sessions,” basically left alone in a comfortable room with a device that gradually administers a measured amount of ketamine intravenously. With this approach, there is little personal interaction between the attending physician or psychiatrist and the client or patient.

I believe that ketamine treatment within the context of the psychotherapy relationship lends itself to way more healing. There is a preparation session, sitting with you while you have the experience, (a three hour session) and then an integration session, in which the therapy work reflects on the power of the experience and integrates it while the neuroplasticity is still accessible.

Ketamine is a dissociative drug and thus, loosens up identification with the "default mode network" — the baseline encoded and scripted ways of thinking and feelings. One way to describe this network of thought and feeling is "the story of me" — the remarkably sturdy narratives about ourselves that we repeat with amazing consistency throughout our days. For example, stories such as "I'm not good enough," "There's something wrong with me," "Nobody ever gives me a break," "You can't trust people," etc.

When properly administered, ketamine induction has a powerfully (though temporarily) quieting effect on such ruminations — which results in a temporary (though often profound) freedom from the emotional pain and fear that so tenuously accompanies these habitual thoughts and beliefs.

It is said that "ketamine is the doorway into the workshop." The psychotherapy work that follows is the workshop in which the integration of the journey can powerfully contribute to internalizing a lasting transformative impact.

How does it work? If interested, we explore the idea together. If we decide this is a good idea, I will refer you to the psychiatrist that I have teamed up with for this purpose. You will meet with him and if it is decided that you are a good candidate for this treatment, you will receive a prescription of a sublingual dose. After our preparation sessions, you will bring the medicine with you to your session and I will stay with you as you ingest it in your mouth, letting it swish around your mouth and holding it for ten to fifteen minutes before swallowing. I will hold a safe container for you as you journey, which lasts for about an hour to an hour and a half. We will have plenty of time for you to come back into your body and for us to make sure you feel okay and in tact before leaving. It is recommended that on the day you take the ketamine, you do not drive that day but rather set up assistance to get home. Twenty-four or forty eight hours after the ketamine session, we will have an integration session.

Thus the term "ketamine-assisted psychotherapy" (or "KAP," for short) refers to not only being with you as you have this experience but also to the process of learning to consciously and deliberately apply and integrate the expanded perspectives that arise from ketamine-induced "journeys."

If you are contemplating ketamine-assisted therapy with me, please understand that, except under exceptional circumstances, the actual introduction of ketamine into our work would only be an option after at least a month or two of non-drug-assisted preparatory psychotherapy. This establishes our connection as the safe container and deepens my understanding of you so that I am better able to assist you in integrating the experience afterwards.
