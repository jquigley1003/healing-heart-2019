Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy

I am excited to be certified to offer Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy.

Although Ketamine is known primarily as an anesthetic, it is now FDA approved for the treatment of depression, alcohol and substance dependence, food addiction, OCD, PTSD and other trauma related disorders.

Although it is not effective for everyone — and to be clear it is not in itself a "cure" -- Ketamine treatment is opening doors to breakthroughs in healing for many people, as it exerts antidepressant effects by enhancing neuroplasticity (meaning, the ability of brain cells to form new connections with one another and new pathways). The glutamate pathway of ketamine is very different from that used by other psychiatric dugs, which is part of what makes its impact unlike other forms of treatment.

With ketamine assisted psychotherapy, the recommended treatment usually involves 5 or 6 administrations of ketamine, once a week or every other week, with an integration session following each ketamine session or every other session. This is a very different approach from the "ketamine infusion clinics" in which one goes for 6 or more weekly or bi-weekly "infusion sessions," where one is basically left alone in a comfortable room with a device that gradually administers a measured amount of ketamine intravenously. With this approach, there is little personal interaction between the attending physician or psychiatrist and the client or patient.

I believe that ketamine treatment within the context of the psychotherapy relationship lends itself to much deeper healing. There is a preparation session, then sitting with you while you have the experience (a three hour session), and then an integration session, in which our therapy work focuses on the power of the experience, and helping you to integrate it while the ketamine-induced neuroplasticity is still accessible.

Ketamine is a dissociative drug and thus loosens up identification with our "default mode network" — our baseline encoded and scripted ways of thinking and feeling. One way to describe this network of thought and feeling is "the story of me" — the remarkably sturdy narratives about ourselves that we repeat with amazing consistency throughout our days. For example, stories such as "I'm not good enough," "There's something wrong with me," "Nobody ever gives me a break," "You can't trust people," etc.

When properly administered, ketamine induction has a powerfully (though temporarily) quieting effect on such ruminations — which results in a temporary (though often profound) freedom from the emotional pain and fear that so tenuously accompanies these habitual thoughts and beliefs.

How does this KAP process work? If interested, we explore the idea together. If we decide this is a good idea for you at this time, I will refer you to a ketamine-wise psychiatrist. You will meet with him or her and if it is decided that you are a good candidate for this treatment, you will receive a prescription for several sublingual lozenge doses. After our preparation sessions, you will bring the medicine with you to your session and I will stay with you as you ingest it, letting it swish around in your mouth for a period of ten to fifteen minutes before swallowing. I will hold a safe container for you as you journey, which lasts for about an hour to an hour and a half. We will have plenty of time for you to come back into your body and for us to make sure you feel okay and intact before leaving. It is recommended that you not drive on the day you take the ketamine, but rather set up assistance to get home. Within twenty-four to forty eight hours after the ketamine session, we will have an integration session.

Thus the term "ketamine-assisted psychotherapy" (or "KAP," for short) refers to not only being with you as you have this experience but also to the process of learning to consciously and deliberately apply and integrate the expanded perspectives that arise from ketamine-induced "journeys."

If you are contemplating ketamine-assisted therapy with me, please understand that, except under exceptional circumstances, the actual introduction of ketamine into our work would only be an option after at least a month or two of non-drug-assisted preparatory psychotherapy. This establishes our connection as the safe container and allows me to deepen my understanding of you so that I am better able to assist you in integrating the experience afterwards.
